Worm

A type of malicious software that replicates itself to spread to other computers.

Description

In cybersecurity, a 'worm' is a standalone piece of malware that is capable of self-replication and distribution without the need for a host program or human intervention. Unlike viruses, which attach themselves to legitimate programs and require user action to spread, worms exploit vulnerabilities in operating systems or applications to propagate across networks. Once a worm infiltrates a system, it can consume bandwidth and resources, create backdoors for other malware, and potentially lead to data breaches. Worms often spread via email attachments, downloads, and network shares. A well-known example of a worm is the 'ILOVEYOU' worm, which spread through email in 2000 and caused widespread damage. Modern worms can also leverage social engineering techniques to trick users into executing them, making them a significant threat to both individual users and organizations. Effective measures against worms include regular software updates, firewalls, and antivirus solutions.

Examples

  • ILOVEYOU worm - spread via email in 2000, causing billions in damages.
  • Conficker worm - infected millions of computers globally, exploiting vulnerabilities to spread.

Additional Information

  • Worms can sometimes carry additional payloads, such as ransomware or spyware.
  • Network segmentation and intrusion detection systems can help mitigate the risks posed by worms.
